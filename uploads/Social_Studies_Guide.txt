WORLD HISTORY PERIODS
================================================================================

ANCIENT CIVILIZATIONS (3000 BCE - 500 BCE)
Mesopotamia:
- Located between Tigris and Euphrates rivers (modern Iraq)
- Sumer: first civilization, city-states, cuneiform writing
- Babylonia: Code of Hammurabi (first written law code)
- Assyria: military empire, administrative systems
- Key contributions: writing, laws, wheel, mathematics, irrigation

Ancient Egypt:
- Centered on Nile River (modern Egypt)
- Three kingdoms: Old Kingdom (pyramids), Middle, New
- Pharaohs: divine rulers, centralized government
- Social structure: nobles, merchants, farmers, slaves
- Key contributions: hieroglyphics, mathematics, medicine, architecture
- Monuments: Great Pyramid, temples, tombs

Indus Valley Civilization:
- Harappa and Mohenjo-daro (modern Pakistan/India)
- Planned cities with sophisticated drainage systems
- Undeciphered script (still being studied)
- Trade networks across Asia
- Mysteriously declined around 1900 BCE

Ancient China:
- Yellow River ("Cradle of Chinese Civilization")
- Dynasties: Xia, Shang, Zhou
- Shang Dynasty: bronze work, oracle bones, early writing
- Zhou Dynasty: feudalism, Mandate of Heaven
- Philosophical schools: Confucianism, Daoism, Legalism
- Key contributions: writing, bronze, silk, agriculture

Mesoamerican Civilizations:
- Olmec: mother culture (1500-400 BCE)
- Maya: mathematics, astronomy, calendar, writing
- Aztec: empire, Tenochtitlan, tribute system
- Key contributions: calendar, ball games, agriculture (maize)

CLASSICAL PERIOD (500 BCE - 500 CE)
Ancient Greece:
- City-states (polis): Athens, Sparta
- Athens: democracy, philosophy, democracy, theater
- Sparta: militaristic, oligarchy, discipline
- Classical period: Pericles, Socrates, Plato, Aristotle
- Arts and culture: sculpture, literature, Olympic Games
- Key contributions: democracy, philosophy, drama, science
- Persian Wars: Greeks defend against Persian Empire
- Peloponnesian Wars: Athens vs Sparta

Roman Empire:
- Republic (509-27 BCE): Senate, consuls, expansion
- Empire (27 BCE-476 CE): Augustus to fall
- Military: legions, conquest, expansion across Mediterranean
- Government: intricate system of checks and balances
- Law: Roman law (foundation for modern legal systems)
- Engineering: aqueducts, roads, Colosseum, Pantheon
- Culture: Latin language, literature (Virgil, Ovid), law
- Decline: military pressures, internal strife, invasions

Hellenistic Period:
- Alexander the Great: conquest, spread of Greek culture
- Hellenism: blending of Greek and Eastern cultures
- Intellectual advancement: mathematics, astronomy, medicine

MEDIEVAL PERIOD (500 - 1500 CE)
Early Middle Ages:
- Fall of Western Roman Empire (476 CE)
- Rise of Germanic kingdoms
- Byzantine Empire: continuation of Eastern Rome (1000+ years)
- Islamic Golden Age: science, mathematics, philosophy

Feudalism:
- Decentralized political system
- Feudal hierarchy: king, nobles, knights, peasants
- Manorial system: self-sufficient estates
- Feudal contract: loyalty for protection
- Knights: military class, code of chivalry

Medieval Church:
- Catholic Church: spiritual and political power
- Monasteries: preserved knowledge, education
- Clergy: educated class
- Crusades: religious wars for Jerusalem
- Inquisition: religious authority enforcement

African Kingdoms:
- Mali: Mansa Musa, Timbuktu, Islamic learning
- Songhai: West African empire
- Zimbabwe: great stone structures

Asian Civilizations:
- Islamic Caliphates: Baghdad, Cairo
- Ottoman Empire: rise of Turkish empire
- China: Song and Ming dynasties, technological advancement
- Japan: feudalism, samurai culture

THE RENAISSANCE (1300s-1600s)
Definition:
- "Rebirth" of Classical learning and culture
- Transition from Medieval to Modern period

Characteristics:
- Humanism: emphasis on human potential and achievement
- Patronage: wealthy families support artists and scholars
- Scientific inquiry: questioning, observation, experimentation
- Break from Church authority in intellectual matters

Geography:
- Started in Italian city-states (Florence, Venice)
- Spread across Europe
- Centers: Rome, Florence, Venice

Key Figures and Contributions:
- Leonardo da Vinci: art, science, engineering
- Michelangelo: sculpture, architecture, painting
- Raphael: painting, architecture
- Machiavelli: political philosophy (The Prince)
- Petrarch: literature, humanism

Technology and Exploration:
- Printing press (Gutenberg): spread of ideas
- Improved navigation instruments
- Gunpowder weaponry
- Exploration enabled by improvements

AGE OF EXPLORATION (1400s-1700s)
Motivations:
- "Gold, God, Glory" - wealth, religious conversion, national pride
- Trade route expansion
- Technological improvements

Key Explorers:
- Portuguese: Prince Henry, Vasco da Gama
- Columbus: crossing Atlantic, "discovery" of Americas
- Magellan: circumnavigation
- French, English, Dutch explorers

Exploration Results:
- European colonization of Americas, Africa, Asia
- Columbian Exchange: crops, animals, diseases, cultures
- Global trade networks
- Devastation of indigenous populations through disease and conquest

INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTION (1750-1840s)
Prerequisites:
- Agricultural improvements: increased food production
- Available capital: wealth from trade, colonization
- Resources: coal, iron, raw materials
- Technology development

Key Innovations:
- Steam engine: James Watt (power source for factories)
- Textile machine: spinning jenny, water frame, power loom
- Iron and steel production: better manufacturing
- Transportation: locomotives, steamships

Economic Changes:
- Factory system: mass production, division of labor
- Capitalism: private ownership, profit motive
- Class structure shift: rise of industrial bourgeoisie
- Wealth creation and concentration

Social Changes:
- Urbanization: migration to cities for factory work
- Poor working conditions: long hours, child labor, dangerous
- Living conditions: crowded cities, disease, pollution
- Labor movements: trade unions, workers' rights
- Education: more literacy, demand for workers

MODERN ERA (1800s-early 1900s)
Nationalism:
- Identification with nation-state
- Unification movements: Italy, Germany
- Imperialism: powerful nations control others
- Colonial empires: competition for global resources

Industrial Society:
- Advanced capitalism and mass production
- Class conflict: bourgeoisie vs proletariat (workers)
- Socialist and communist ideas develop
- Labor reforms gradually improve conditions

WORLD WAR I (1914-1918)
Causes:
- Militarism: buildup of military power
- Alliance system: complex web of alliances
- Imperialism: competition for colonies
- Assassination: Archduke Franz Ferdinand triggers war

Key Features:
- Trench warfare: static lines, massive casualties
- New weapons: machine guns, poison gas, tanks, aircraft
- Total war: civilians involved, economies mobilized
- Stalemate for years before eventual Allied victory

Consequences:
- Millions dead, economies destroyed
- Treaty of Versailles: harsh on Germany
- Rise of nationalism and resentment
- League of Nations: first international organization (failed)

INTERWAR PERIOD (1918-1939)
Great Depression:
- Stock market crash (1929)
- Widespread unemployment, poverty
- Governments struggle with response
- Rise of extremist political movements

Rise of Fascism:
- Mussolini in Italy: dictatorship, military aggression
- Hitler in Germany: Nazi party, Aryan supremacy, racial hatred
- Military expansion: Italy in Ethiopia, Germany in Rhineland, Austria
- Appeasement: Britain and France try to avoid conflict

WORLD WAR II (1939-1945)
Causes:
- Nazi Germany's expansion under Hitler
- Japanese militarism in Asia
- Italian aggression
- Failure of League of Nations

Key Events:
- 1939: Germany invades Poland (starts war in Europe)
- 1941: Germany invades Soviet Union; Japan attacks Pearl Harbor
- Holocaust: systematic genocide of 6 million Jews, millions others
- Major battles: Stalingrad, D-Day (Normandy invasion), Iwo Jima

Outcome:
- Germany and Japan defeated
- Atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki
- ~50 million deaths total
- Cold War emerges between USA and Soviet Union

CONTEMPORARY ERA (1945-present)
The Cold War (1945-1991):
- Ideological conflict: capitalism (US) vs communism (USSR)
- No direct military conflict between superpowers
- Proxy wars: Korea, Vietnam, Afghanistan
- Nuclear arms race: weapons of mass destruction
- Space race: competition for technological superiority
- Divided world: NATO vs Warsaw Pact

Decolonization:
- Former colonies gain independence
- India, Africa, Southeast Asia become independent nations
- Liberation movements: Gandhi (non-violent), Ho Chi Minh, others
- New nations face challenges of development

Modern Globalization:
- International trade: multinational corporations
- Technology: internet, communication networks
- Cultural exchange: worldwide sharing of media, ideas
- Environmental awareness: climate change, sustainability

Recent Decades:
- Fall of Soviet Union (1991): end of Cold War
- US as sole superpower
- Rise of China and India as economic powers
- Terrorism: 9/11 attacks, global war on terror
- Information age: computers, internet, social media

GOVERNMENT AND POLITICAL SYSTEMS
================================================================================

TYPES OF GOVERNMENT
Monarchy:
- Ruled by single person (king/queen)
- Absolute: monarch has unlimited power
- Constitutional: monarch limited by constitution
- Hereditary succession

Democracy:
- Rule by the people
- Direct democracy: citizens vote on every issue (ancient Athens)
- Representative democracy: citizens elect representatives (most modern)
- Principles: majority rule, protection of minorities, rule of law

Oligarchy:
- Rule by small group (wealthy, military, nobles)
- Limited participation in government
- Power concentrated in few hands

Dictatorship/Autocracy:
- Single person with absolute power
- No elections or limited control
- Often maintained by military or police state
- Examples: Hitler, Stalin, current authoritarian regimes

Totalitarianism:
- Government controls all aspects of life
- Single party, suppression of opposition
- Propaganda and information control
- Mass surveillance

POLITICAL SYSTEMS
Presidential System:
- President: head of state and government
- Separation of powers: executive, legislative, judicial
- Legislative branch: elected by people
- Example: United States, Brazil, Mexico

Parliamentary System:
- Prime minister: head of government (chosen by parliament)
- Monarch or president: ceremonial head of state
- Government drawn from parliament
- Elections can change government quickly
- Example: UK, Canada, Australia, Germany

Federal Systems:
- Power divided between national and state/provincial governments
- States have own governments and policies
- National government handles defense, currency, treaties
- Examples: USA, Canada, Germany, India

Unitary Systems:
- Centralized power in national government
- Local divisions have limited authority
- Changes from national government affect all regions
- Example: France, Japan, most other countries

RIGHTS AND FREEDOMS
Human Rights:
- Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UN)
- Rights inherent to all people
- Dignity, equality, freedom from torture
- Freedom of religion, speech, assembly

Civil Rights:
- Rights within a specific country
- Protection against discrimination
- Equal treatment under law
- Voting rights

Fundamental Freedoms:
- Freedom of speech: express opinions
- Freedom of religion: worship and belief
- Freedom of assembly: gather peacefully
- Freedom of press: publish information

Bill of Rights (US Example):
1. Freedom of speech, religion, press, assembly
2. Right to bear arms
3. No quartering of soldiers
4. Protection against unreasonable searches
5. Due process, self-incrimination protection
6. Right to speedy trial
7. Right to jury trial
8. No cruel and unusual punishment
9. Other rights retained by people
10. Powers to states and people

LAW AND JUSTICE
Criminal Law:
- Violations against society (theft, assault, murder)
- Prosecuted by government
- Defendant must be proven guilty "beyond reasonable doubt"
- Penalties: fines, imprisonment, community service

Civil Law:
- Disputes between individuals or organizations
- Lower standard of proof: "preponderance of evidence"
- Remedies: compensation, injunctions, contracts enforcement

Constitutional Law:
- Interpretation of constitution
- Supreme court role: judicial review
- Balancing individual rights and government power

GOVERNING STRUCTURE
Separation of Powers:
- Executive: enforces laws, president/prime minister, cabinet
- Legislative: makes laws, parliament/congress
- Judicial: interprets laws, courts, judges

Checks and Balances:
- Each branch limits power of others
- President can veto legislation
- Congress can override veto (supermajority)
- Senate confirms judges
- Courts can declare laws unconstitutional

INTERNATIONAL RELATIONS
Diplomacy:
- Negotiation between nations
- Embassies and ambassadors
- Treaties and agreements
- Peaceful conflict resolution

Alliances:
- NATO: North Atlantic Treaty Organization (military alliance)
- EU: European Union (political and economic union)
- Regional alliances: ASEAN, African Union

Organizations:
- United Nations: international peacekeeping and cooperation
- World Trade Organization: international commerce
- International Court of Justice: disputes between nations

Geopolitics:
- Power and influence between nations
- Competition for resources and territory
- Spheres of influence
- National interests guide policy

================================================================================
ECONOMICS FUNDAMENTALS
================================================================================

BASIC CONCEPTS
Scarcity:
- Resources are limited, wants are unlimited
- Forces choices in production and consumption
- Economics studies how to allocate scarce resources

Opportunity Cost:
- Value of next best alternative given up
- Every choice has a cost
- Essential to decision-making

Supply and Demand:
- Supply: amount producers willing to sell at various prices
- Demand: amount consumers willing to buy at various prices
- Equilibrium: supply equals demand (market price)
- Price increases if demand > supply
- Price decreases if supply > demand

Market Structure:
- Perfect competition: many buyers/sellers, no one controls price
- Monopoly: one seller, controls market and price
- Oligopoly: few large firms dominate market
- Monopolistic competition: many sellers with differentiated products

ECONOMIC SYSTEMS
Capitalism:
- Private ownership of resources and means of production
- Market forces determine prices and quantities
- Profit motive drives innovation
- Consumer choice directs production
- Advantages: efficient, innovative, high productivity
- Disadvantages: inequality, market failures, externalities
- Example: USA, Japan, Singapore

Socialism:
- Collective or state ownership of resources
- Government planning determines production
- Goal: reduce inequality, meet basic needs
- Disadvantages: less efficient, less innovation
- Example: Soviet Union, Cuba, Venezuela, North Korea

Mixed Economy:
- Combination of capitalism and socialism
- Private and public ownership
- Market with government regulation
- Most modern economies are mixed
- Example: USA, Germany, Sweden, China

PRODUCTION
Factors of Production:
- Land: natural resources
- Labor: human effort and skills
- Capital: tools, equipment, facilities
- Entrepreneurship: innovation, risk-taking, organization

Productivity:
- Output per unit of input
- Increases with technology, training, better management
- Higher productivity = higher wages and economic growth

Specialization:
- Focus on what you do best
- Division of labor: each person does specific task
- Increases efficiency and production
- Basis of trade between countries

MONETARY POLICY
Money:
- Medium of exchange, store of value, unit of account
- Reduces transaction costs compared to barter
- Central banks manage money supply

Inflation:
- Rise in general price level over time
- Reduces purchasing power of money
- Causes: excess money supply, rising input costs
- Moderate inflation (2-3%) is healthy
- Hyperinflation (>50% per month) is destructive

Interest Rates:
- Cost of borrowing money
- Set by central bank (Federal Reserve in USA)
- Higher rates: economic slowdown, save more
- Lower rates: economic stimulus, borrow more

Central Banking:
- Federal Reserve (USA), ECB (Europe), etc.
- Controls money supply and interest rates
- Regulates banks, maintains financial stability

FISCAL POLICY
Taxation:
- Government revenue from citizens and businesses
- Progressive tax: higher earners pay higher percentage
- Regressive tax: same percentage for all (hits poor harder)
- Types: income tax, sales tax, corporate tax, capital gains
- Tax policy affects investment, consumption, inequality

Government Spending:
- Defense, education, infrastructure, social programs
- Influences economic growth and inflation
- Multiplier effect: spending creates income for others

Budgets and Deficits:
- Budget deficit: government spends more than it receives
- National debt: accumulated deficits over time
- Structural deficit: deficit during normal economic times
- Cyclical deficit: temporary from recession

TRADE AND COMMERCE
Imports and Exports:
- Import: goods from foreign countries
- Export: goods sold to foreign countries
- Trade balance: exports minus imports
- Trade deficit: imports exceed exports
- Trade surplus: exports exceed imports

Comparative Advantage:
- Countries specialize in what they do better
- Each country still benefits from trade
- Based on opportunity costs, not absolute advantage

Tariffs and Trade Barriers:
- Tariff: tax on imported goods
- Protectionism: limiting imports to help domestic industry
- Arguments for: protect jobs, infant industries, national security
- Arguments against: raises prices, reduces competition, inefficient

Globalization:
- Increasing international trade and investment
- Multinational corporations operate worldwide
- Supply chains span continents
- Offshoring: moving production to lower-cost countries

ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT
GDP (Gross Domestic Product):
- Total value of goods and services produced
- Measures economic size
- Higher GDP means larger economy
- Growing GDP indicates economic growth

Economic Growth:
- Increases in GDP over time
- Driven by productivity, innovation, investment
- Sustainable growth without depleting resources

Poverty and Inequality:
- Absolute poverty: can't meet basic needs
- Relative poverty: below standard for country
- Income inequality: measured by Gini coefficient
- Development: process of improving living standards

Global Inequality:
- Developed vs developing nations
- Access to education, healthcare, infrastructure
- Colonial legacy affects current development
- Development aid and foreign investment

GEOGRAPHY ESSENTIALS
================================================================================

PHYSICAL GEOGRAPHY
Continents:
- North America: temperate, developed economies
- South America: tropical, biodiversity, developing economies
- Europe: temperate, developed, densely populated
- Africa: tropical/subtropical, developing, resources
- Asia: largest, diverse climates, rising economies
- Australia: arid, island continent
- Antarctica: frozen, no permanent settlements

Oceans:
- Pacific: largest, borders Americas, Asia, Australia
- Atlantic: connects Europe, Africa, Americas
- Indian: borders Africa, Asia, Australia
- Arctic: frozen, climate change affecting
- Movement: currents, tides, waves

Landforms:
- Mountains: formed by plate tectonics, erosion
- Valleys: carved by rivers, glaciers
- Plains: flat areas, often fertile
- Deserts: dry regions, limited precipitation
- Coasts: transition between land and sea

Climate Zones:
- Tropical: hot, high precipitation
- Subtropical: warm, some seasonal variation
- Temperate: moderate, four seasons
- Polar: extremely cold
- Determined by latitude, ocean currents, elevation

Biomes:
- Tropical rainforest: highest biodiversity, rapid cycling
- Temperate forest: seasonal, moderate biodiversity
- Grassland/prairie: low trees, grazing animals
- Tundra: frozen, permafrost, low precipitation
- Desert: minimal water, sparse vegetation
- Aquatic: freshwater (rivers, lakes), marine (oceans)

HUMAN GEOGRAPHY
Population:
- Distribution: uneven across globe
- Density: people per unit area
- Growth: births minus deaths plus migration
- Demographic transition: from high to low birth/death rates

Migrations:
- Internal: within country
- International: between countries
- Push factors: war, poverty, persecution
- Pull factors: opportunity, safety, family
- Economic migration: seeking better livelihood
- Forced migration: slavery, refugees

Urbanization:
- Growth of cities and urban populations
- Rural to urban migration
- Megacities: 10+ million people
- Challenges: overcrowding, infrastructure, services
- Opportunities: economic growth, cultural exchange

Cultures and Societies:
- Language: shapes how we think and communicate
- Religion: spiritual beliefs and practices
- Customs and traditions: ways of life
- Social norms: acceptable behavior
- Acculturation: adoption of different culture

CULTURAL GEOGRAPHY
Ethnicity and Identity:
- Ethnic groups: shared culture, language, heritage
- Nationalism: identification with nation-state
- Diaspora: dispersion of ethnic groups
- Multiculturalism: diversity within society

Religion:
- Christianity: 2+ billion followers (Catholicism, Protestantism, Orthodox)
- Islam: 1.8+ billion followers (Sunni, Shia)
- Hinduism: 1+ billion followers (India, Nepal)
- Buddhism: 500+ million followers (East, Southeast Asia)
- Judaism: 15+ million followers (Israel, diaspora)
- Others: Sikhism, Jainism, Shinto, etc.

POLITICAL GEOGRAPHY
Borders and Sovereignty:
- Borders: lines dividing countries
- Sovereignty: supreme authority within territory
- Territorial disputes: competing claims
- International law: boundaries based on treaties

Geopolitics:
- Geography influences political relationships
- Access to resources shapes power
- Strategic locations valuable (Suez Canal, Panama Canal)
- Regional powers compete for influence

MAP SKILLS
Latitude and Longitude:
- Latitude: distance north/south of equator (0-90°)
- Longitude: distance east/west of prime meridian (0-180°)
- Grid system for locating places

Map Projections:
- Mercator: uses lines/angles correctly, distorts size at poles
- Equal-area: preserves size, distorts shape
- Robinson: compromise between accuracy and appearance

Thematic Maps:
- Population distribution
- Climate zones
- Economic activity
- Political boundaries
- Topography (elevation)

GOVERNMENT AND CIVICS (PRACTICAL)
================================================================================

CITIZENSHIP RIGHTS AND RESPONSIBILITIES
Rights:
- Vote and participate in government
- Freedom of expression and religion
- Equal protection under law
- Trial by jury

Responsibilities:
- Obey laws
- Pay taxes
- Serve on juries
- Defend country when needed
- Stay informed and participate

LEVELS OF GOVERNMENT
Local:
- City/County level
- Services: police, fire, schools, roads
- Elected officials: mayor, city council, county commissioners

State/Provincial:
- Mid-level government
- Services: education, highways, state police
- Elected officials: governor, state legislature

National/Federal:
- Entire country
- Services: defense, foreign policy, interstate commerce
- Elected officials: president, members of congress

US GOVERNMENT STRUCTURE (EXAMPLE)
Legislative Branch (Congress):
- Senate: 100 members (2 per state), 6-year terms
- House of Representatives: 435 members, 2-year terms
- Powers: make laws, approve budget, declare war, impeach

Executive Branch:
- President: chief executive, commander-in-chief
- Vice President: succeeds president if needed
- Cabinet: heads of major departments
- Bureaucracy: agencies implementing policy
- Powers: enforce laws, command military, negotiate treaties

Judicial Branch:
- Supreme Court: 9 justices, interprets constitution
- Lower courts: district and appellate courts
- Powers: interpret laws, review constitutionality
- Judicial review: courts can strike down unconstitutional laws

DEMOCRATIC PROCESS
Voting:
- Secret ballot: privacy protected
- Universal suffrage: all adults can vote
- Equal representation: one person, one vote
- Voter registration: citizens must register

Elections:
- Campaigns: candidates present platforms
- Debates: public discussion of issues
- Polling: predict outcomes, influence campaigns
- Term limits: prevent indefinite rule (US president: 2 terms)

CONSTITUTIONS AND LAWS
Constitution:
- Framework for government
- Defines powers and rights
- Hard to change (requires amendment)
- Interpreted by courts

Laws:
- Congress makes laws (statutes)
- President can veto
- Courts interpret laws
- Enforcement: police, prosecutors

POLITICAL PARTIES
Two-Party System (US):
- Democrats: generally support larger government, social programs
- Republicans: generally support smaller government, lower taxes
- Some argue two parties limit choice

Multi-Party Systems:
- More than two significant parties
- Coalition governments common
- Each party represents specific ideology
- Example: European democracies

CIVIC PARTICIPATION
Voting:
- Most direct form of participation
- Influences policy directly
- Local elections often have low turnout

Community Service:
- Volunteer work
- Jury duty
- Civic organizations
- Town halls and public meetings

Activism:
- Protest and advocacy
- Petition and lobbying
- Civil disobedience (nonviolent rule-breaking)
- Example: Civil Rights Movement, environmental activism

GLOBAL CITIZENSHIP
Sustainability:
- Meeting current needs without harming future generations
- Environmental protection
- Renewable resources
- Reduce, reuse, recycle

Cultural Understanding:
- Learn about different cultures
- Respect differences
- Challenge stereotypes and prejudice
- Cross-cultural communication

Social Justice:
- Equality for all people
- Fair treatment regardless of identity
- Addressing systemic discrimination
- Human rights advocacy

PRACTICE QUESTIONS
================================================================================

History:
Q1: What were the three main causes of World War I?
Answer: Militarism, alliance system, imperialism, assassination of Franz Ferdinand

Q2: Describe the feudal system.
Answer: Hierarchy of king, nobles, knights, and peasants with mutual obligations

Q3: What were the Crusades?
Answer: Religious wars to reclaim Holy Land for Christianity

Government:
Q4: Name three branches of government and their roles.
Answer: Executive (enforce laws), Legislative (make laws), Judicial (interpret laws)

Q5: What is checks and balances?
Answer: System where each branch limits power of others

Q6: Explain direct democracy vs representative democracy.
Answer: Direct = citizens vote on issues; Representative = citizens elect representatives

Economics:
Q7: What is opportunity cost?
Answer: Value of next best alternative given up when making a choice

Q8: Explain supply and demand and how they determine price.
Answer: Price rises if demand > supply, falls if supply > demand

Q9: What is comparative advantage?
Answer: Countries specialize in what they produce at lowest opportunity cost

Geography:
Q10: What are the three largest continents by population?
Answer: Asia, Africa, Europe

Q11: Describe how climate affects human settlement.
Answer: Fertile, moderate climates draw populations; extremes limit settlements

Q12: What is urbanization and why does it happen?
Answer: Growth of cities from rural migration for economic opportunities

Civics:
Q13: What is the difference between a right and a responsibility?
Answer: Right = freedom granted; Responsibility = duty citizens must fulfill

Q14: Why do we have elections?
Answer: Determine who leads government, express citizen preferences, enable change

Q15: What does it mean to be a global citizen?
Answer: Understanding cultural differences, supporting sustainability, promoting justice

STUDY STRATEGIES FOR SOCIAL STUDIES
================================================================================
- Timelines: build chronological chains to connect cause/effect
- Compare/Contrast: use T-charts for systems (democracy vs autocracy), events, regions
- Spatial Thinking: sketch quick maps for wars, trade routes, resources
- Flashcards & Retrieval: key terms, dates, people; spaced repetition
- Teach-Back: explain topics aloud to test understanding
